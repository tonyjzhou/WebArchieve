    Big Short hero’s cautionary tale

    Michael Burry made a fortune from the subprime crash and became famous due to the book and movie that followed, but he couldn't extract good returns from a failing retailer of men's suits.

    Aug 4, 2020 – 11.33am
     or Subscribe to save article

    Of all the characters in The Big Short, Michael Lewis’ wonderful book about the investors who profited from the subprime crash, few were quite as colourful as Michael Burry.

    The former doctor with the glass eye was a maverick figure with whom many small investors could identify. He was the self-taught value investor portrayed as a lone wolf who spotted cracks in a system that was making everyone else rich – and then found a way to win in the face of huge odds and strident criticism.

    Burry and his firm Scion Capital disappeared for a while following his huge GFC win, but after a brief detour into water trading, they re-emerged with a focus on Burry’s first love – value investing in small caps.

    But as can happen when you’re playing in this end of the market at a time of high volatility, not every bet is going to come off.

    And that’s exactly what Burry discovered on Monday night when his bet on US men’s clothing retailer Tailored Brands met an unfortunate end, with the group filing for Chapter 11 bankruptcy protection.

    Burry first grabbed a stake in Tailored Brands, which owns the Men’s Wearhouse and Jos. A. Bank chains, way back in 2016.

    But in the past 12 months, his conviction around Tailored Brands and the value he believed was trapped in it grew stronger – even though this was a business that has looked like a dog for a long time.

    Founded in 1973 by a Houston entrepreneur called George Zimmer – who was apparently known across the US for his wonderfully 1980s slogan "You're going to like the way you look. I guarantee it" – Men’s Wearhouse became the place an average Joe could go to find the inevitable suit at the back of the closet.

    But it first hit turbulence in 2013. Zimmer, then executive chairman, was forced out amid tension with the board and as the pressure on the retail sector from e-commerce started to rise.

    Undaunted, Men’s Wearhouse acquired upmarket chain Jos. A. Banks in a 2014 deal worth almost $US1.8 billion.

    The acquisition would take the group’s store network to 1400 and its employee count to 18,000. But while Tailored Brand’s shares would peak at $US64 each in 2015 – they closed at just US30¢ on Monday night – it quickly became apparent that putting two struggling retailers together and loading the company up with debt was not smart.

    The increasing casualisation of workplaces, and the shift to online, saw Tailored Brands’ sales fall every year since 2016, making it difficult for it to deal with its debt load.

    Advertisement

    But Burry saw value. Remarkably, the company kept investors sweet with $US72¢ of dividends each year, worth $36.4 million in total.

    Burry wrote several activist letters to the board last year, imploring it to dump the dividend and instead make share buybacks to lift earnings per share.

    He backed up his point with share purchases. In September last year, his stake grew to 2.25 million shares or 4.45 per cent. At the end of 2019, he owned 3.3 million shares.

    By March this year, he had a stake of 8.3 per cent (4 million shares), even as he warned Bloomberg that he had turned bearish on the market.

    It’s a cautionary tale of the dangers of trying to catch a falling knife.

    “If you are in stocks because they have been going up and because the central banks always could apply the brakes to any sell-off, well, those preconditions are not currently valid,” Burry said on March 12.

    Under the restructuring proposal announced on Monday night, Tailored Brands plans to reduce debt by $US360 million, cut 20 per cent of management jobs and close 500 stores.

    The deal has the support of 75 per cent of its lenders, who will get $US325 million to $US425 million and 100 per cent of the equity in the reorganised entity. Existing shareholders will be wiped out.

    Advertisement

    Burry reduced his holding to less than 5 per cent in June. Whether he got out completely isn’t clear.

    Still, you might argue that Burry is unlucky and that without a pandemic that has made suits – and, indeed, going to the office – a distant memory, he may have been able to force change at Tailored Brands and extract enough value to make a decent return.

    But it’s a cautionary tale of the dangers of trying to catch a falling knife.

    When a company finds itself in serious trouble – growing debt, steadily falling sales – even small shocks can be damaging. And big shocks can be fatal.

    If there’s another lesson to extract from this collapse, it’s that Burry was right – sort of – when he said there is a limit to what central banks can do.

    Sure, the Federal Reserve has propped up markets through its extraordinary interventions. But the Fed still can’t create the economic demand real businesses like Tailored Brands need to survive.

    The bankruptcies and defaults in the US are far from done.
